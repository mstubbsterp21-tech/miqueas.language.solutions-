Summer School for Accessibility: Why Your Organization Needs Professional ASL Support This Season

The month of June often marks a transition in the professional world. As schools recess and organizations shift into summer programming, workshops, and planning phases, communication access remains a primary responsibility. This season provides a unique window for institutions to evaluate their current accessibility standards and ensure they are prepared for the high-stakes communication needs of the upcoming year.

In St. Petersburg and the surrounding Florida regions, June 2026 is a period of active learning. Local institutions like the Family Center on Deafness are hosting their "Summer 2026 ASL Classes" (running Mondays from June 8 to July 13), and Seminole State College continues its tradition of rigorous summer ASL coursework.

While these community classes are excellent for fostering basic awareness and cultural interest, they highlight a critical distinction: the difference between foundational signing and professional communication access. For organizations, summer is the time to bridge that gap.

1. Why Summer is the Optimal Season for Accessibility Planning

Professional communication access is not a service that should be requested as an afterthought. It requires foresight, an understanding of the environment, and a commitment to ethical standards.

Strategic Review of Communication Protocols

Summer allows administrators and HR professionals the "quiet" time necessary to conduct a credential review of their existing service providers. Relying on "availability" rather than "readiness" can lead to miscommunication in critical moments. Using this time to establish a relationship with a qualified interpreter ensures that when the busy fall season arrives, your organization is already aligned with a professional who understands your specific modality and cultural needs.

Preparing Public-Facing Content

Many organizations utilize the summer months to update their digital presence. This is an ideal time to consider ASL translation services for public-facing video content. Translating English transcripts into professional ASL videos ensures that your message is accessible to the Deaf community before your next major campaign or school semester begins.

Helpful Guidance: Readiness vs. Availability
A professional interpreter does not just "show up." They prepare by reviewing materials, understanding the goals of the interaction, and ensuring they are the right "fit" for the specific communication needs of the Deaf consumer.

2. The Impact of Qualified Educational Interpreters

Educational settings, whether they are K-12 classrooms, university lectures, or corporate training workshops, demand a specific level of technical skill and ethical judgment.

Micah Stubbs, the founder of Miqueas Language Solutions, operates as a qualified interpreter with an EIPA 3.9 score and over 7 years of extensive experience in diverse interpreting environments. In the field of educational interpreting, these metrics serve as a benchmark for clarity and accuracy.

Understanding the EIPA Standard

The Educational Interpreter Performance Assessment (EIPA) is a rigorous standard that evaluates an interpreter’s ability to transmit classroom content accurately while maintaining the linguistic integrity of both English and ASL. A score of 3.9 indicates a high level of proficiency, ensuring that the student or participant receives the same quality of information as their hearing peers.

Professional Standards and Ethics

Maintaining a firm commitment to the NAD-RID Code of Professional Conduct as well as NAIE professional standards is not optional. In educational and professional settings, an interpreter must navigate complex dynamics with humility and professional distance. This ensures the focus remains on the communication between the participants, not the interpreter.

Why NAIE Professional Standards Matter in School Settings

The National Association of Interpreters in Education (NAIE) professional standards are essential in educational interpreting because they define the specific roles, boundaries, and responsibilities of working within a school system. Educational interpreters do not function in isolation. They work within a team structure that may include teachers, speech therapists, administrators, support staff, and families, all while keeping the student’s communication access at the center of the process.

Following NAIE standards helps interpreters collaborate more effectively with school personnel and students by clarifying expectations around preparation, role boundaries, classroom participation, and ongoing professional judgment. This matters because the goal is not simply for a Deaf student to be physically present in the room. The goal is for that student to have meaningful access to instruction, peer interaction, and the full educational process as an active participant.

For a qualified interpreter such as Micah Stubbs, an EIPA 3.9 score and 7+ years of experience support that standard of practice, but technical skill alone is not enough. Educational interpreting also requires ethical consistency, respect for team-based collaboration, and careful attention to the student’s actual communication needs in each setting.

3. Beyond Basic Signs: Professional Communication Access

There is a common misconception that anyone who has completed a basic ASL series, such as those offered this June at the Family Center on Deafness or Seminole State College, can facilitate professional communication.

While community learning is vital for building a more inclusive society, it does not replace the need for ASL interpreter services.

The Limits of Conversational Proficiency

An individual with basic signing skills may understand 300 to 500 signs, but they often lack the "prosody" (the rhythm and stress of the language), the grammatical depth, and the cultural responsiveness required for professional settings. In a classroom or a business meeting, misinterpreting a single technical term can have lasting consequences for the Deaf consumer’s access to information.

Why Your Organization Needs a Specialist

Professional communication access involves:

  1. Modality Matching: Ensuring the interpreter’s signing style matches the specific linguistic needs of the consumer.
  2. Contextual Preparation: Analyzing the vocabulary and goals of a workshop or meeting beforehand.
  3. Ethical Judgment: Knowing when to step back or when a different type of support (such as a Deaf-Blind specialist) is required.

Many people searching for a "certified sign language interpreter" are actually seeking the assurance that their communication will be handled with precision and care. By choosing a qualified professional with proven experience, your organization demonstrates a commitment to genuine human connection rather than a transactional checkbox.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. What is the difference between a community signer and a qualified interpreter?
A community signer may have conversational skills, but a qualified interpreter has undergone rigorous assessment (such as the EIPA) and adheres to a professional code of ethics. Professional interpreters possess the cognitive ability to process two languages simultaneously while maintaining accuracy and cultural responsiveness.

2. Why should we plan for ASL support during the summer?
Summer is an ideal time for a "credential review" of your accessibility providers. It allows you to establish a partnership with a qualified interpreter before high-stakes events occur in the fall, ensuring the professional is familiar with your organization’s terminology and culture.

3. Does an EIPA score matter for non-school settings?
Yes. While the EIPA is designed for educational settings, the skills it measures: clarity, accuracy, and linguistic integrity: are highly transferable to any workshop, training session, or professional presentation where information must be conveyed precisely.
