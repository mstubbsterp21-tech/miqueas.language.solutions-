When On-Site is the Professional Answer

There are specific settings where VRI may not be the correct tool. As a qualified interpreter with an EIPA 3.9 score and over 7 years of experience, Micah Stubbs has seen first-hand how some environments require physical presence and fuller access to the room.

  1. High-stakes conversations: When information is complex, sensitive, or consequential, an in-person interpreter is often better equipped to follow the full communication environment.
  2. Touch-based communication needs: Some consumers use communication methods that require direct physical access, which cannot be provided through a remote video screen.
  3. Highly interactive settings: Group discussion, frequent turn-taking, visual materials, demonstrations, and shared space can make on-site interpreting the stronger option.
  4. Young learners: Some children may not have the visual stamina or language development to maintain access through a two-dimensional screen for long periods.

Helpful Guidance: Assessing Readiness Readiness is more than just being available. It means having the specific cultural responsiveness and linguistic skill required for that specific setting. If the technical or environmental factors are not right, the ready choice is to switch to on-site.
