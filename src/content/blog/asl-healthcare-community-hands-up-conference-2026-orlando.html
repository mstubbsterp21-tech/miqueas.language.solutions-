The Credential Review Process

When selecting a professional ASL interpreter, institutions should look beyond mere certification labels. A true professional is defined by their ongoing practice, their ethical judgment, and their specific qualifications. For instance, an EIPA 3.9 score reflects a high level of proficiency in educational and community settings, which is a vital metric for assessing an interpreter’s skill set in complex environments.

Professionalism also involves knowing when a "fit" is not right. A humble, authoritative interpreter will admit when a specific assignment requires expertise outside their current scope. This level of honesty is what protects the integrity of the communication process and the safety of the Deaf consumer.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. How do I know if an interpreter is qualified for a medical setting?

A qualified interpreter will have a proven track record (7+ years of experience), high performance scores (such as an EIPA 3.9), and a firm commitment to the NAD-RID Code of Professional Conduct. In medical settings, they should also demonstrate a mastery of medical terminology and an understanding of patient confidentiality (HIPAA).

2. Is VRI an appropriate substitute for in-person interpreting at a conference?

While Video Remote Interpreting (VRI) has its place in certain telehealth or quick consultations, in-person interpreting is often the standard for conferences and complex medical screenings like those at a mobile clinic. In-person services allow for better visual clarity, cultural nuance, and the ability to navigate physical environments effectively.

3. What should our organization provide to the interpreter before the conference starts?

Preparation is key to accuracy. Providing the interpreter with schedules, presentation materials, or specific terminology lists allows them to prepare the linguistic equivalent in ASL. This ensures a higher level of clarity for the Deaf consumer.
