In the modern landscape of accessibility, video remote interpreting services (VRI) have become a standard tool for many organizations. The appeal is clear: immediate connection, reduced travel costs, and the ability to bridge gaps in areas where local interpreters may be scarce. However, a professional ASL interpreter understands that convenience must never come at the expense of effective communication.

Choosing the right "modality": the method through which interpreting is delivered: is not a simple administrative decision. It is an ethical responsibility that requires a thorough review of the environment, the participants, and the specific goals of the interaction. At Miqueas Language Solutions LLC (MLS), we believe that centering the needs of the Deaf consumer is the only way to ensure true communication access.

The Ethical Standard: Centering the Consumer

The foundation of any interpreting assignment is the NAD-RID Code of Professional Conduct. This standard dictates that interpreters must provide service in a manner appropriate to the situation.

When an organization defaults to VRI without consulting the individuals involved, they risk creating a barrier rather than removing one. Effective access is not just about having a screen in the room; it is about ensuring the Deaf participant can fully engage, contribute, and understand without undue stress or technical interference.

Helpful Guidance: The "Default" Rule As a qualified interpreter, Micah Stubbs recommends defaulting to on-site services whenever the interaction involves high-stakes information, complex group dynamics, or when the Deaf consumer explicitly expresses a preference for in-person support.

Technical Requirements for Effective VRI

If VRI is determined to be a suitable fit after a professional assessment, several technical standards must be met. Without these, the quality of ASL communication access will suffer, leading to miscommunication.

1. Stable High-Speed Internet

A reliable connection is the backbone of VRI. For video to be clear enough to see the nuances of sign language: facial expressions, hand shapes, and body shifts: a bandwidth of at least 1.5 Mbps (up/down) is necessary, though 3-5 Mbps is preferred for high-definition clarity. A wired Ethernet connection is always superior to Wi-Fi to prevent "freezing" or "lag."

2. Clear Audio for Spoken Language Users

The interpreter must hear the spoken English clearly to translate it accurately into ASL. If the room has poor acoustics, background noise, or multiple people speaking away from the microphone, the interpreter’s work becomes significantly more difficult. Organizations should use high-quality external microphones rather than relying on built-in laptop mics.
